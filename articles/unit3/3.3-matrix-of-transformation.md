# The Matrix of a Transformation

_From geometric description to numbers in a grid._

---

## The Recipe

We established a powerful fact in the last section:

> **To find the matrix of a linear transformation, just figure out where the standard basis vectors go.**

Let's practice this skill. It's one of the most useful techniques in linear algebra.

---

## Example 1: Reflection Across a Line

Suppose we want the matrix for reflection across the line $y = x$ in 2D.

**Step 1: Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ go?**

The vector pointing right, when reflected across $y = x$, lands pointing up:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ go?**

The vector pointing up, when reflected across $y = x$, lands pointing right:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Assemble the matrix**

The images become columns:
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

## Example 2: Projection onto a Line

Let's find the matrix for projecting onto the line $y = 2x$ (the line through the origin with slope 2).

A unit vector along this line is:
$$\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

But we don't need to normalize for finding where basis vectors go. Let's think geometrically.

**Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ go?**

Project the point $(1, 0)$ onto the line $y = 2x$. Using the projection formula (or geometry):
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 1/5 \\ 2/5 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ go?**

Project $(0, 1)$ onto $y = 2x$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 2/5 \\ 4/5 \end{bmatrix}$$

**The matrix:**
$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 1/5 & 2/5 \\ 2/5 & 4/5 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Practice Problem:** Find the matrix for reflection across the $x$-axis.

Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ go?

$$\text{(A) } \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{(B) } \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{(C) } \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{(D) } \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Check your answer</strong></summary>

**(B)**

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

A point on the $x$-axis stays on the $x$-axis when you reflect across the $x$-axis. The vector $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ is unchanged.

The second basis vector $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ goes to $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$ (flipped vertically).

So the matrix is $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$.

</details>

## Example 3: Rotation

Find the matrix for rotation by angle $\theta$ counterclockwise.

**Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ go?**

The point $(1, 0)$ rotated by $\theta$ lands at $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta \\ \sin\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

**Where does $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ go?**

The point $(0, 1)$ rotated by $\theta$ lands at $(-\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} -\sin\theta \\ \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

**The rotation matrix:**
$$R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

## Example 4: 3D Rotation About the $z$-axis

In 3D, rotating about the $z$-axis leaves $z$-coordinates unchanged and rotates in the $xy$-plane.

**Where do the basis vectors go?**

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta \\ \sin\theta \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} -\sin\theta \\ \cos\theta \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**The matrix:**
$$R_z(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Notice the 2×2 rotation matrix in the top-left, with 1 in the bottom-right for the unchanged $z$-axis.

## Composing Transformations

What if you want to do *two* transformations in sequence?

First apply $T_1$ (matrix $A$), then apply $T_2$ (matrix $B$).

The combined transformation is $T_2(T_1(\mathbf{x})) = B(A\mathbf{x}) = (BA)\mathbf{x}$.

**Composition corresponds to matrix multiplication!**

This is one of the deep reasons why matrix multiplication is defined the way it is.

<details>
<summary><strong>Order matters!</strong></summary>

Note that we apply $A$ first, then $B$, but the product is $BA$ (not $AB$).

This matches function composition: $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$ applies $f$ first.

Matrix multiplication is generally not commutative: $AB \neq BA$.

</details>

---

**Practice Problem:** You rotate by 90° counterclockwise, then reflect across the $x$-axis. Which transformation is this equivalent to?

The rotation matrix is $R = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$.

The reflection matrix is $F = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$.

What is the matrix for the combined transformation?

$$\text{(A) } \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{(B) } \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{(C) } \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{(D) } \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

<details>
<summary><strong>Check your answer</strong></summary>

**(A)**

$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

We need $FR$ (reflection after rotation):

$$FR = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is reflection across the line $y = -x$.

</details>

## Translating Geometry to Algebra

Here's a summary of how to turn geometric descriptions into matrices:

| Transformation | How to find the matrix |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Reflection | Find where basis vectors land after reflection |
| Rotation | Use trigonometry ($\cos$, $\sin$) |
| Projection | Project basis vectors onto the target subspace |
| Scaling | Basis vectors scale by appropriate factors |
| Composition | Multiply matrices (right-to-left) |

## The Inverse Question

Given a matrix, we can figure out what transformation it does.

But sometimes we want to go the other direction: given a transformation, find its matrix.

Even more interesting: given a matrix, can we find the **inverse transformation** — the one that undoes it?

Not every transformation is reversible. Projection squishes space flat; you can't un-squish it. But rotations, reflections, and stretches (with nonzero factors) are reversible.

This leads us to **matrix inverses**, which we'll explore in a later section.

---

## Summary

- **To find a transformation matrix:** Determine where each standard basis vector lands, then use those as columns
- **Rotation by θ:** $\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$
- **Reflection across y=x:** $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$
- **Reflection across x-axis:** $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$
- **Composition:** To apply $A$ then $B$, multiply as $BA$
- **3D rotations:** Put the 2D rotation in the right place, with 1s for the unchanged axis
